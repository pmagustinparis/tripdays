How Many Days in Tokyo?

7-10 days recommended

Why Visit Tokyo?

Tokyo, Japan's dynamic capital, is a city of fascinating contrasts where ancient traditions harmoniously coexist with cutting-edge innovation. This sprawling metropolis offers an unparalleled urban experience with its neon-lit skyscrapers, historic temples, world-class museums, and peaceful gardens. As a global leader in technology, fashion, and pop culture, Tokyo constantly reinvents itself while honoring its rich heritage. The city's legendary food scene ranges from Michelin-starred restaurants to humble street food stalls, all united by an unwavering commitment to quality and presentation. Tokyo's distinct neighborhoods each have their own character - from the youthful energy of Shibuya and the luxury boutiques of Ginza to the traditional atmosphere of Asakusa and the otaku paradise of Akihabara. With its efficient public transportation, impeccable cleanliness, and the legendary Japanese hospitality, Tokyo offers visitors a seamless blend of excitement, culture, and wonder that makes it one of the world's most captivating destinations.

How Many Days Do You Need in Tokyo?

To properly experience Tokyo, we recommend 7-10 days. This duration allows you to explore the city's diverse neighborhoods, visit major attractions, enjoy the food scene, and perhaps take a day trip to nearby destinations like Kamakura or Hakone. If you only have 4-6 days, you can still see the highlights, but you'll need to be selective and focus on specific areas. With more than 10 days, you can discover Tokyo at a more relaxed pace and venture further afield to places like Nikko or even Kyoto.

Tokyo Itinerary: Day by Day

Day 1: Tokyo Essentials

Begin your Tokyo adventure with some of its most iconic sights. Start at the Imperial Palace and its beautiful East Gardens (note that the inner palace grounds are only open to the public on specific days). Continue to Tokyo Station to admire its restored historic facade and perhaps explore the Character Street shopping area inside. Head to Ginza, Tokyo's upscale shopping district, and visit the Kabukiza Theatre for a single-act kabuki performance if time permits. End your day at Tokyo Tower for spectacular night views of the illuminated city.

Day 2: Traditional Tokyo

Explore Tokyo's traditional side. Begin at Asakusa to visit the ancient Senso-ji Temple, Tokyo's oldest Buddhist temple, and walk through the atmospheric Nakamise Shopping Street leading to it. Continue to Ueno Park, home to several museums including the excellent Tokyo National Museum with its extensive collection of Japanese art and antiquities. In the afternoon, visit the peaceful Yanaka neighborhood with its traditional wooden houses, small temples, and artisan shops, one of the few areas that survived the bombings of WWII.

Day 3: Modern Tokyo

Experience Tokyo's contemporary face. Start at Shibuya to witness the famous Shibuya Crossing, the world's busiest pedestrian intersection, and visit the Hachiko Statue. Explore the trendy Harajuku district, walking down Takeshita Street with its youth fashion and crepe shops. Continue to the elegant Omotesando avenue, often called Tokyo's Champs-Élysées. In the afternoon, visit Shinjuku to see the impressive Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building with its free observation decks, and explore the bustling Shinjuku Station area, the world's busiest railway station.

Day 4: Otaku and Electronics

Dive into Tokyo's otaku (geek) culture and electronics scene. Begin in Akihabara, the famous electronics district and center of anime and manga culture. Explore the multi-story electronics stores, anime shops, and perhaps visit a maid café for a unique experience. Continue to Ikebukuro to see another side of otaku culture, particularly popular with female fans. Visit Sunshine City for shopping and entertainment, including the Pokémon Center and Namjatown indoor theme park. End your day in Nakano Broadway, a shopping mall filled with stores selling anime merchandise, vintage toys, and collectibles.

Day 5: Tokyo Bay Area

Explore Tokyo's futuristic waterfront. Start at Odaiba, a man-made island in Tokyo Bay with futuristic architecture, shopping malls, and museums. Visit TeamLab Borderless, an immersive digital art museum, and the Miraikan (National Museum of Emerging Science and Innovation). See the Gundam Statue outside DiverCity Tokyo Plaza and enjoy views of Tokyo Bay and Rainbow Bridge. Take a ride on the Yurikamome Line automated transit system for great views of the bay area. End your day at Toyosu Market, the new home of Tokyo's famous fish market, for dinner featuring the freshest seafood.

Day 6: Parks and Gardens

Enjoy Tokyo's green spaces. Begin at Shinjuku Gyoen National Garden, one of Tokyo's most beautiful parks with Japanese, English, and French garden sections. Continue to the Meiji Shrine, a serene Shinto shrine set in a forested area in the heart of Tokyo, and walk through the adjacent Yoyogi Park, especially lively on weekends. In the afternoon, visit the Institute for Nature Study, a natural forest preserve in the city, or the Koishikawa Korakuen, one of Tokyo's oldest and most beautiful landscape gardens. End your day with a stroll through Inokashira Park in Kichijoji, a popular spot for cherry blossom viewing in spring.

Day 7: Day Trip to Kamakura

Take a day trip to Kamakura, a coastal town about an hour from Tokyo. Once the political center of Japan, Kamakura is home to numerous temples, shrines, and the famous Great Buddha (Daibutsu), a bronze statue over 13 meters tall. Visit the impressive Tsurugaoka Hachimangu Shrine, the Hokokuji Temple with its bamboo garden, and take a stroll along the Komachi-dori shopping street. If time permits, hike part of the Daibutsu Trail for beautiful views of the surrounding area. Return to Tokyo in the evening.

Day 8: Day Trip to Hakone

Venture to Hakone, a mountainous area known for its hot springs and views of Mount Fuji. Purchase the Hakone Free Pass for unlimited use of the area's transportation. Take the Hakone Tozan Railway (mountain railway), the Hakone Ropeway for aerial views, and a cruise across Lake Ashi on a pirate ship-styled boat. Visit the Hakone Open-Air Museum with its impressive outdoor sculpture collection and the Hakone Shrine with its iconic "torii" gate in the lake. If time permits, relax in one of the area's many hot springs (onsen). Return to Tokyo in the evening.

Day 9: Tokyo's Art Scene

Immerse yourself in Tokyo's vibrant art scene. Begin at the Tokyo National Museum of Modern Art in Kitanomaru Park to see works by leading Japanese artists. Continue to Roppongi to visit the Mori Art Museum on the 53rd floor of Mori Tower, which offers contemporary art exhibitions and spectacular city views from the Tokyo City View observation deck. Explore the National Art Center, Tokyo, one of Japan's largest art venues with changing exhibitions. In the afternoon, visit smaller galleries in areas like Kiyosumi-Shirakawa, which has become a hub for contemporary art spaces. End your day in Daikanyama, a stylish neighborhood with boutiques, cafes, and the architecturally striking Daikanyama T-Site bookstore complex.

Day 10: Hidden Tokyo

On your final day, discover some of Tokyo's lesser-known treasures. Begin in Shimokitazawa, a bohemian neighborhood known for vintage shops, independent boutiques, and a thriving music scene. Continue to Kagurazaka, once a geisha district, with its French-influenced cafes and traditional Japanese restaurants down atmospheric alleyways. Visit the Nezu Museum with its excellent collection of Japanese and Asian art and beautiful garden. Explore the upscale residential area of Daikanyama with its stylish shops and cafes. End your Tokyo adventure with a farewell dinner in the atmospheric Ebisu or Meguro neighborhoods, known for their excellent restaurants and local atmosphere.

Top Attractions in Tokyo

  • Senso-ji Temple - Tokyo's oldest Buddhist temple in Asakusa
  • Meiji Shrine - Serene Shinto shrine set in a forested area

Practical Tips for Visiting Tokyo

Best Time to Visit

The best times to visit Tokyo are late March to early April for cherry blossoms, and October to November for autumn colors and pleasant weather. Spring and fall offer mild temperatures and beautiful seasonal scenery. Summer (June-August) can be hot and humid with occasional typhoons, while winter (December-February) is cold but relatively dry with fewer tourists. Consider avoiding major Japanese holidays like Golden Week (late April to early May) and New Year when many businesses close and domestic tourism peaks.

Getting Around

Tokyo has an extensive and efficient public transportation system. The subway and JR trains connect all parts of the city, though the network can be complex for first-time visitors. Purchase a Suica or Pasmo IC card for convenient payment on all trains and buses. Consider a Tokyo Subway Ticket for unlimited subway rides if you'll be using public transport extensively. The Yamanote Line is particularly useful as it loops around central Tokyo, connecting major stations and neighborhoods. Taxis are clean and reliable but expensive; use them sparingly. Walking is ideal for exploring specific neighborhoods, but distances between areas are often too great to walk.

Budget Planning

Tokyo can be expensive, but there are ways to manage costs. Expect to spend about $100-150 per day per person for mid-range travel, including accommodation, meals, attractions, and transportation. Save money by staying in business hotels or hostels, eating at casual restaurants like ramen shops and conveyor belt sushi, and visiting free attractions like parks and certain temples. Budget accommodations start around $50 per night, while mid-range hotels range from $100-200. Consider purchasing a Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building has free observation decks with city views comparable to paid towers.

Local Etiquette

Japanese culture values politeness and consideration. Bow slightly when greeting people, and remove shoes when entering homes, traditional ryokans, and some restaurants. Avoid eating while walking or talking loudly on public transportation. Tipping is not customary and can even cause confusion. When using chopsticks, never stick them vertically into rice (this resembles a funeral ritual) and don't pass food directly from chopsticks to chopsticks. Learn basic phrases like "Arigatou gozaimasu" (thank you) and "Sumimasen" (excuse me/sorry) - they'll be appreciated.

Is Tokyo Worth Visiting?

Tokyo is absolutely worth visiting for its unique blend of ultramodern and traditional experiences. Few cities in the world offer such a fascinating contrast between cutting-edge technology and ancient traditions, all functioning in perfect harmony. The city's exceptional food scene, efficient infrastructure, distinctive neighborhoods, and the politeness of its people create an environment that's both exciting and comfortable for visitors. While Tokyo can be overwhelming at first due to its size and complexity, the rewards of exploring this dynamic metropolis are immense. Whether you're interested in technology, traditional culture, food, shopping, or simply experiencing one of the world's most unique urban environments, Tokyo delivers an unforgettable experience that justifies its reputation as one of the world's greatest cities.
