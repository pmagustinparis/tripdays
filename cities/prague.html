How Many Days in Prague?

3-5 days recommended

Why Visit Prague?

Prague, the "City of a Hundred Spires," captivates visitors with its fairy-tale atmosphere, where Gothic, Baroque, Renaissance, and Art Nouveau buildings create one of Europe's most beautiful urban landscapes. The Czech capital's historic center, a UNESCO World Heritage site, has miraculously survived wars and revolutions with its medieval layout and ancient architecture largely intact. Straddling the Vltava River, Prague offers a perfect blend of history and modernity, where you can cross the 14th-century Charles Bridge in the morning, explore the largest ancient castle complex in the world by afternoon, and enjoy world-class jazz in a contemporary venue by night. The city delights with its hidden courtyards, winding cobblestone streets, and unexpected vistas that reveal themselves around every corner. Beyond its architectural splendor, Prague boasts a rich cultural heritage, from classical music to puppet theaters, and a renowned beer culture with some of the world's finest brews. With its central European location, reasonable prices, and welcoming atmosphere, Prague provides an enchanting experience that combines old-world charm with vibrant contemporary life.

How Many Days Do You Need in Prague?

To properly experience Prague, we recommend 3-5 days. This duration allows you to visit the major attractions, explore different neighborhoods, enjoy the food and beer scene, and perhaps take a day trip to nearby destinations like Kutná Hora or Karlštejn Castle. If you only have 1-2 days, you can still see the highlights, but you'll need to be selective and focus on specific areas. With more than 5 days, you can discover Prague at a more relaxed pace and venture further afield to places like Český Krumlov, Karlovy Vary, or even neighboring cities like Vienna or Dresden.

Prague Itinerary: Day by Day

Day 1: Prague Castle and Lesser Town

Begin your Prague adventure with its most iconic landmark. Start at Prague Castle (Pražský hrad), the largest ancient castle complex in the world and the official residence of the Czech president. Arrive early to avoid crowds and witness the Changing of the Guard ceremony at noon. Explore the castle grounds, including the magnificent St. Vitus Cathedral with its stunning stained glass windows, the Old Royal Palace, St. George's Basilica, and the charming Golden Lane with its tiny, colorful houses. After lunch, descend into the picturesque Lesser Town (Malá Strana) with its Baroque palaces and churches. Visit the beautiful St. Nicholas Church with its impressive dome and bell tower. Stroll through Wallenstein Garden with its geometric layout and peculiar statues. End your day by walking across the iconic Charles Bridge (Karlův most) with its 30 statues of saints, enjoying the sunset views of the Vltava River and Prague Castle.

Day 2: Old Town and Jewish Quarter

Dedicate this day to Prague's historic center. Begin at the Old Town Square (Staroměstské náměstí), one of Europe's most beautiful urban spaces, surrounded by colorful historic buildings. Watch the Astronomical Clock (Orloj) strike the hour with its procession of apostles (on the hour from 9 AM to 11 PM). Visit the Gothic Church of Our Lady before Týn with its distinctive twin spires and the Old Town Hall. Continue to the Jewish Quarter (Josefov) to explore its synagogues and the hauntingly beautiful Old Jewish Cemetery with graves layered up to 12 deep due to space limitations. Visit the Spanish Synagogue with its stunning Moorish interior and the Old-New Synagogue, Europe's oldest active synagogue. In the afternoon, walk along Paris Street (Pařížská), Prague's most exclusive shopping avenue. End your day with a traditional Czech dinner in one of the Old Town's historic restaurants, perhaps trying classics like goulash, svíčková (marinated beef), or roast duck with dumplings and sauerkraut.

Day 3: New Town and Vyšehrad

Explore Prague beyond the medieval center. Begin at Wenceslas Square (Václavské náměstí), the commercial heart of Prague and site of many historic events, including the Velvet Revolution. Visit the National Museum at the top of the square or the Museum of Communism to learn about the city's recent history. Continue to the New Town (Nové Město), which despite its name dates back to the 14th century. See the unusual Dancing House (Tančící dům), a modern building resembling a pair of dancers. In the afternoon, visit Vyšehrad, a historic fort on a hill overlooking the Vltava River, with beautiful gardens, the neo-Gothic Church of St. Peter and Paul, and a cemetery where many famous Czech personalities are buried. Enjoy panoramic views of Prague from the ramparts. End your day with a sunset river cruise on the Vltava, seeing Prague's illuminated landmarks from a different perspective.

Day 4: Day Trip to Kutná Hora

Take a day trip to Kutná Hora, a UNESCO World Heritage site about an hour east of Prague by train. This medieval silver mining town was once the second most important city in the Bohemian Kingdom. Visit the magnificent St. Barbara's Church, a Gothic masterpiece built by the wealthy miners. Explore the Italian Court (Vlašský dvůr), a former royal residence and mint where the Prague groschen coins were once produced. The town's most unusual attraction is the Sedlec Ossuary (Bone Church), a small chapel decorated with the bones of an estimated 40,000-70,000 people, arranged into chandeliers, coats of arms, and other decorations. Return to Prague in the evening and experience the city's beer culture by visiting a traditional Czech beer hall like U Fleků, brewing beer since 1499, or one of the modern craft beer establishments that have flourished in recent years.

Day 5: Art and Alternative Prague

On your final day, discover Prague's artistic and alternative side. Begin at Prague's National Gallery in the Veletržní Palace, housing an impressive collection of modern and contemporary art, including works by Picasso, Monet, and Czech artists like Mucha and Kupka. Visit the DOX Centre for Contemporary Art in the Holešovice district, a converted factory that hosts innovative exhibitions. Explore the vibrant Holešovice neighborhood, once an industrial area now transformed with galleries, design shops, and cafes. See the John Lennon Wall, filled with Beatles-inspired graffiti and messages of peace since the 1980s. Visit Kampa Island and the Museum Kampa with its collection of Central European modern art. End your Prague adventure by exploring the trendy Žižkov or Vinohrady neighborhoods, known for their local atmosphere, numerous pubs, and restaurants away from the tourist crowds. Enjoy a farewell dinner at a local restaurant, perhaps accompanied by Czech beer or wine from the Moravian region.

Top Attractions in Prague

  • Prague Castle - World's largest ancient castle complex with St. Vitus Cathedral
  • Charles Bridge - 14th-century stone bridge with statues and street artists
  • Old Town Square - Historic square with the Astronomical Clock and Gothic churches
  • Jewish Quarter - Historic area with synagogues and the Old Jewish Cemetery
  • Lesser Town - Picturesque district with Baroque palaces and churches
  • Wenceslas Square - Commercial and cultural center of the New Town
  • Vyšehrad - Historic fort with beautiful views and the national cemetery
  • Dancing House - Modern architectural landmark resembling dancers
  • Petřín Hill - Park with a lookout tower resembling the Eiffel Tower
  • Municipal House - Art Nouveau gem with concert hall and restaurants

Practical Tips for Visiting Prague

Best Time to Visit

The best times to visit Prague are during spring (April to May) and fall (September to October) when the weather is mild and crowds are smaller. Summer (June to August) brings warm temperatures but also the largest crowds and higher prices. Winter (November to March) can be cold with occasional snow, but offers magical Christmas markets in December and generally lower prices. Prague is particularly beautiful when dusted with snow, though some attractions may have reduced hours. Major events include the Prague Spring Music Festival (May-June), Christmas Markets (December), and the Bohemian Carnival (February).

Getting Around

Prague has an excellent public transportation system. The metro, trams, and buses are efficient, affordable, and reach all parts of the city. Purchase a 24-hour, 72-hour, or weekly pass for unlimited travel. The historic center is compact and best explored on foot, though be prepared for cobblestone streets and comfortable walking shoes are essential. Taxis are available but use only official companies or ride-hailing apps like Uber or Bolt to avoid overcharging. Prague is built on hills, so be prepared for some uphill walking, particularly around Prague Castle and Vyšehrad. River cruises offer a relaxing way to see the city from a different perspective.

Budget Planning

Prague is more affordable than many Western European capitals. Expect to spend about 1500-2500 CZK ($60-100) per day per person for mid-range travel, including accommodation, meals, attractions, and transportation. Save money by eating at local restaurants away from tourist areas, using public transportation, and taking advantage of free attractions like parks, churches (when not during services), and walking tours. Budget accommodations start around 500-1000 CZK ($20-40) per night, while mid-range hotels range from 1500-3000 CZK ($60-120). The Prague Card offers free entry to many attractions and unlimited public transport, which may be worthwhile if you plan to visit multiple paid sites.

Local Etiquette

Czechs are generally reserved but friendly. Greet people with a simple "Dobrý den" (Good day) when entering small shops or restaurants. Tipping is customary in restaurants - 10% is standard for good service, though not obligatory as in the US. Round up the bill for taxis and small purchases. When visiting churches, dress modestly and maintain a respectful silence. Remove your hat when entering buildings. Czechs are proud of their beer culture - when toasting, maintain eye contact, say "Na zdraví" (To health), and never cross arms with others while clinking glasses. Be aware that many restaurants and cafes in tourist areas may add a service charge - check your bill carefully.

Is Prague Worth Visiting?

Prague is absolutely worth visiting for its extraordinary preservation of historical architecture, rich cultural heritage, and atmospheric charm. Few European cities offer such a complete medieval urban landscape, where you can walk across a 14th-century bridge, explore a thousand-year-old castle, and dine in Gothic cellars all in one day. The city's reasonable prices compared to Western European capitals make it accessible for travelers on various budgets. While Prague can be crowded in the summer months, its many hidden courtyards, lesser-known neighborhoods, and quiet early mornings offer opportunities to experience the city's magic away from the tourist throngs. Whether you're interested in history, architecture, music, art, or simply soaking up the ambiance of one of Europe's most beautiful cities, Prague offers an enchanting experience that continues to captivate visitors from around the world.
